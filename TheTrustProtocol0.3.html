What is The Trust Protocol?

“FIRST, DO NO HARM.” - Hippocrates[g]

The Trust Protocol is a set of concrete recommended actions that any individual or group in the leftist movement can apply to common problem situations that occasionally appear in leftist spaces (though this Protocol may be used by other groups as well). These recommended actions are designed first and foremost to minimize existing harm and prevent further harm, both to the people directly involved in the process, and for the broad leftist movement as a whole. The Protocol also contains a broad code of ethical principles that form the basis for the recommended actions in The Protocol itself.

An advantage of having a named and tangible protocol is that when sticky situations[h][i] arise, you can use it to guide your personal actions in measured and effective responses to some problematic situation you find yourself involved in. There are actionable guidelines for the people directly involved in a damaging claim, and for third-party bystanders who are dragged into (or voluntarily enter) the resulting dispute.

Another advantage is that people who aren’t directly involved in a claim can point at The Trust Protocol as a shorthand way of explaining/defending their actions (or inaction) in response to the situation. Being able to say “hey, we have a protocol for situations like this” can be a very effective tactic for minimizing harm for everyone who is touched somehow[j][k] by a problematic situation.

The more participants within the broad leftist movement that apply The Protocol’s guidelines and ethical principles to our published content, our social commentary, and to our interactions in groups and across social media, the more resilient we become to bad actors who try to divide and conquer us through targeted harassment, derailment, and divisive rhetoric. Voluntary adoption of The Trust Protocol can enable all of us on the left to support each other and minimize harm.

The protocol also gives us a productive way to respond to true claims[l][m][n] that might happen among the broader leftist community. A way to leave the door open for rehabilitation and growth, rather than slowly deleting each other with unrealistically unforgiving purity tests while falling prey to incitement by bad actors.

What The Trust Protocol is NOT

The Trust Protocol is not a weapon. It’s not meant to limit your individual voice, nor to be used as a tool for leftists to silence valid [o][p][q]concerns. Whether you observe the protocol is entirely voluntary at all times. You can try to observe all of the guidelines, or only the ones you agree with.

It’s critical to remember that we are all human, with human failings. None of us deserve to be put on a pedestal, because we are all fallible. We should accept that any of us will make human mistakes now and again, and instead of trying to police each other about those mistakes or trying to gain social capital through callouts about each other’s human mistakes, we should instead focus on positive support and and recognition that all of us are capable of growing and learning from our mistakes.

In crude terms, if we instantly dogpile on each other for some perceived wrong, choose sides, and play all the sectarian politics and competitive games we’re conditioned to play by capitalist society, [r][s][t]we’re simply pushing whichever person into a corner and triggering defenses. In many cases, the broader leftist movement has even fallen for schemes organized by bad actors. It’s incredibly rare that any positive growth comes out of such reactions. In fact, we see time and again that significant harm can occur from such dogpiling and purity-testing—often to bystanders who had nothing to do with the original situation.

The Context in which The Trust Protocol Was Developed[u][v][w]

The Trust Protocol has been developed in era where society is still learning how to recognize and minimize harm done by individuals or society, especially in online spaces. We must acknowledge that no action is isolated, that participating in online disputes can be traumatizing for all people involved, and that we all have a collective duty to minimize harm. Learning effective techniques for online engagement is a part of a process of learning and healing. The effects of online abuse are still largely dismissed. Responding to trauma and allegations of abuse should be done in a thoughtful way that encourages healing and growth. We must collectively aspire to minimize the occurrence of harmful cycles, and to learn how to respond to problems in a more thoughtful way. Through the strength of our personal ethics, we have the potential to influence others to also respond appropriately to harmful situations, and to avoid contributing to a toxic environment of online harassment. We can and should raise the bar for society and for all online communities, influencing them to behave more responsibly. Harassment is unfortunately still commonplace online, and it limits people's ability to engage in prosocial discourse, find community, and connect with others.

I encourage specifically using the words “empathy” and “compassion” and “patience” and “good faith” in your guidelines. These protocols can be used for any form of harm, but Sexual violence is a pervasive issue in all of society. The guidelines in this protocol can contribute to creating spaces which are more sensitive to those who have been harmed generally, and the issue of sexual violence specifically.

  1. You are responsible for yourself first and foremost.[x] Co-producing safer spaces and treating each other well starts with the individual. Remember to take care of your own physical and emotional needs. If you find yourself in a situation that is difficult for you to manage, make sure to rest and take breaks when you need them. Because of the responsibility, and emotional labor of it, be sure to ask for help when you need it, and look for resources or guidance, especially from people who have been in a similar situation.
  2. Make the use of content warnings standard practice in your space. If you know a piece of content is likely to be disturbing and/or distressing for a large amount of people[y], it's always a good idea to warn people so they can decide if that's something they want to encounter.  Trigger warnings are appropriate when discussing events that may cause people who have experienced similar trauma to relive their experience (PTSD flashbacks)[z]. Content warnings are used for other, likely less distressing, content that users may find unsuitable for every environment and audience.
  3. Make information about consent and that debunks misinformation [aa]about sexual violence easily accessible in your space. Accurate information and education contribute to the prevention of sexual violence and harassment in the first place. Information about consent can be found here ( → link (doc unfinished)), and about myths surrounding sexual violence here ( → link (doc unfinished))
  4. Make information about victim blaming easily accessible in your space, refrain from engaging in victim blaming, and address it when you see it happen. Victim blaming shames and silences people who have experienced sexual violence or harassment. Because the myth of victim responsibility is especially insidious it requires a more focused pamphlet, so more information about victim blaming can be found here ( → link (doc unfinished)) 
  5. Make links to resources for people affected by sexual violence and harassment easily accessible in your space, and familiarize yourself with them. Finding the specific and accurate information can be daunting and complex, especially for people who have experienced sexual violence. Being able to point them in the right direction can be extremely helpful. Some information about available resources can be found here ( → link)
  6. Make information on empathetic communication easily accessible in your space. People might be at a loss when it comes to responding to more emotional, especially traumatic, experiences and may unintentionally further the harm with an uninformed response, despite their best intentions. Information on how to communicate with someone responding to such an experience will help create a more accommodating environment for people in your space who’ve had these experiences. You can find more information on helpful and empathetic communication here ( → link (doc unfinished))
  7. Address untoward and invasive behaviour or comments when you see them occurring in your space. Bystander intervention at the earliest possible juncture puts both perpetrators of inappropriate behavior and witnesses on notice that your space does not tolerate harassment or abuse on any level and helps prevent escalation to more extreme forms of harmful behaviour. It also signals anyone who may be a target of this behaviour that their safety and comfort matter, and prevents silencing.
  8. Where possible, create a designated safe area in your space for people who have experienced sexual violence or harassment where they can share and vent about their experiences. Experiences of sexual violence can be very isolating and people affected can often feel alone and misunderstood. Although we should strive for all spaces to be inclusive to survivors,having a place you know is safe for you to share your experiences can be helpful. If you want to establish such an area, make sure you are able to do so securely with the privacy and safety of the people concerned in mind. Information about how can be found here ( → link (doc unfinished)) [ab]
  9. Establish clear rules and guidelines about harassment and abusive behaviour, including links to valuable information mentioned throughout this protocol. Specific attention for and sensitivity to sexual violence and harassment in your space can help decrease its occurrence and creates a more accommodating environment to those affected by it.

NOTE: All members of a given space inherently have the ability to observe the guidelines described in this protocol. People who occupy positions of power in their spaces (such as admins or moderators) and people who may hold more social capital (such as content creators) should consider their increased opportunity to draw attention to and implement these guidelines.[ac]

Protocol One[ad][ae] - If you or someone you know wants to make a claim

The guidelines in Protocol X are generally applicable to this situation too.

==================

Workgroup Note: Let’s get some actionable guidelines in here about what some person suffering from harm should actually do to bring that harm forward and confront the person they feel caused the harm.

=================

  1. Prioritise your own healing. Traumatic events can leave you feeling as though your own well-being is not important. Try to remember that you are worthy of support even if you are struggling to find it. Think about what you need to heal.  Before anything else seek help for your trauma. Some resources can be found here.

  1. Consider using a confidante: the accountability process is inherently emotional and often painful. Finding a person or a pod who you can give your full experience to, work with, and vent to (especially for unhelpful or invective feelings) will help the community by preventing harm from spreading, and can help you greatly in sorting out what you want from this process.
  1. On selecting confidantes:
  1. Make sure your confidantes believe you on your account, or at least exhibit full trust in it, and understand what you want out of the process.
  2. Make sure that they know they are a confidante according to the protocol so that they understand what being your confidante means.
  3. Make sure that the confidantes that you do choose are willing to stick with you through the process so they can tell you as early as possible if they can’t commit..

  1. If you decide to make your claim public and have trouble coming forward on your own or are not sure of your ability to express what happened to you clearly, ask help from a mediator or confidantes. Do not face your abuser alone, regardless of your choice.
  2. Make the claim directly to the person or with a mediator if you cannot countenance direct interaction with the person. You are never required to face your abuser in the accountability process, though you may if you feel it is necessary.
  3. Use specific language in the claim as much as you can, or engage the help of a mediator. This is important in helping the community understand the exact nature of the violation against you. Don’t hesitate to ask for help or consideration.

  1. Make the claim in front of the smallest audience that you feel comfortable being in front of, which might necessarily be your entire community but might not as well.This is to make sure that enough people get your specific account which will work to prevent uncontrolled fallout through peripheral conversation and rumor spreading while also working to prevent dogpiling in internet spaces.

  1. There is no wrong way to share your experience, so long as it aids your healing. However, It is important that the people who see your account know that you are going to discuss sensitive or graphic information. The people around you should know the nature of the experience you are going to share beforehand in order to avoid potential harm to the people who are exposed to it.

  1. If you decide not to confront the person, that is your choice and your healing is what is important. Do not allow someone to guilt or shame your for wanting your healing process to be separated from that person.

  1. If the person refuses accountability cease contact with them immediately and focus on your own healing. If you share digital spaces with them consider blocking them, if you share in person spaces take precautions such as making sure you have a triad buddy system at a minimum, and do not hesitate to contact security or human resources.

  1. If you are a confidante or mediator or other second party, First: make no action without the consent of your Trustee[af]. Try to understand what your trustee wants out of the process and help them find resources that are pertinent to their needs. Let them know your availability and limits as early as possible. The emotional involvement of this process can lead a victim to feel abandoned, so early updates on your ability to give them attention can prevent unintentional harm. If the person who did the harm to your trustee is someone you have a prejudice against for some other reason, then you should work to keep that prejudice out of this accountability process because it can set back the healing process.

Suggestion for addressing the person who harmed/abused you

There is no right or wrong way for you as a survivor to share your experience of abuse. Regardless of when the abuse occurred, you can share the experience in whatever way is best for your healing. This protocol is just a tool to help.

Some of the most important first steps is to care for yourself. You’ve been hurt and will need to dress your wound. One of the ways to do this is to share this experience with a person you feel comfortable with. While it might feel a daunting task, it allows you to share your feelings with another human being and get support.[ag]

  • After having this support, it’s important to assess whether you would like to address your abuser directly or not. Ask yourself: would it be beneficial or provide me with closure to directly address my abuser? Would it be too dangerous due to how manipulative or abusive they are? You are never obligated to face your abuser. You don’t owe them anything and if they are trying to push for a meeting with you, you address them only on your terms. Knowing your own limits and never address them alone, even if you’re under the impression that the harm wasn’t intended, for your own safety and only consider addressing them if you’re 100% sure of your own limits.[ah]
  • If you’ve decided that speaking to your abuser will help with your healing, then you take another assessment of what preparations you need to safely confront your abuser.
  • With your supportive mediator/pod, the next steps should be to make sure that the(se) individual(s) are prepared to support you through the process and will not attempt to speak for you without your consent. For more information about pods see the resources [here]. Some tips for characteristics to look for in your pod/support are below.
  • The(se) individual(s) who would best suit your pod, will need to know what you want from this confrontation. That way they can try to provide support that will further your goal.
  • Preferably these individuals would not have prejudices against the alleged abuser, but if they do, will be able to reserve them for your sake, since it could invoke further conflict and worsen any trauma/pain.
  • Be careful not to include individuals that do not believe you or gaslight you. If they’re in your support group, they would ineffectively support you and possibly worsen the situation.[ai]
  • If this is impossible, then immediately avoid further interaction with the abuser. Focus on providing yourself with a safe environment.
  • If you’re aware that you share open digital spaces with them, block them.
  • If you share closed digital spaces with them, or refer to indirect address method.[aj]
  • If there are events, such as conventions, that they would likely appear at, take precautions in case there is a face to face meeting such as finding close friends to attend with you, alerting people that such an incident may occur, and if necessary alert security. [ak]

  • After the confrontation, assess whether the confrontation was helpful or not, and whether further steps need to be taken for the healing process.

Protocol A - If someone has made a damaging claim about YOU

The guidelines in Protocol C, Protocol D, Protocol G, Protocol H , and Protocol X are generally applicable to this situation too. It’s also important to minimize potential harm to both yourself and the person making the claim by “thinking like a 3rd-party mediator” and applying many of the guidelines in Protocol B.

  1. Resist giving a knee-jerk reaction. If you are the subject of a damaging claim that goes against general leftist principles and ideals, STOP and think. Take a step back and put yourself in the shoes of the person making the claim. Might you have not understood the conditions that made your behavior unintentionally harmful? Do you understand the perspective of the claimant or claimants? Even though a claim will likely indicate that you have done something that goes against leftist principles, having done so does not necessarily make you an irredeemable reactionary and you might have taken responsibility and changed yourself since material contained in the claim. We all have to take responsibility for any harm we might have caused others, no matter how far in the past, and show that we have been made accountable for that harm, or are willing to be accountable. If you respond in a knee jerk way it will almost definitely be used to attack you.

  1. When an accusation of abuse or harassment has been brought forward it is essential that the YOU DO NOT contact the claimant in a way that they have not approved of. After putting out an initial message requesting their mediator contacts you through a medium they find suitable, you should refrain from publicly discussing them. This includes  not contacting their family, friends, coworkers, and associates with regards to the claim without those people being agreed upon channels of communication. This is integral to the healing and accountability processes. If membership in a group with people who fit those descriptions makes that impossible the consider taking a hiatus from that group, not necessarily leaving. You should answer any questions by saying you are acting through The Protocol to reach accountability with the claimant to resolve the issue.
  2. You have the right to not respond to the claim. Nobody has the right to expect or demand a response from you, whether publicly or privately. There are plenty of legitimate reasons you may have for not being able to engage, but you should also be aware that actions (and inactions) have consequences and both the claimants and bystanders may have concerns and are just as free to criticize your silence as you are to maintaining it.
  3. You have a right to voluntarily leave a leftist space if a damaging claim has been made about you and you do not wish to go through an accountability process. A moderator should inform you that you may not be allowed back in at a later date, as it was your choice not to face accountability for the issue, and it is unreasonable to expect a group with unfixed membership and goals to simply re-visit an old allegation where key information may no longer be available.
  4. If you accept accountability for the actions in the claim The terms of any method to peaceable resolution should be defined only by the involved individuals. Resolution and accountability should be expressed in terms that both parties understand and agree to, as well as being aligned with the other statements in this protocol.
  5. If additional claims are added to a claim for which you are already performing an accountability process. If these are made by the original claimants then you and they should decide if the current process is sufficient. If it is brought up by new claimants then they should find out if the accountability process that is currently being undertaken is sufficient for them. Both cases should find or support accountability which includes the new claims..


Protocol B - If you are a 3rd party voluntarily trying to help people affected by a damaging claim

The guidelines in Protocol D, Protocol E, and Protocol F are generally applicable to this situation too.

  1. Use “I’m sorry you’re going through this” if approached by the claimant, or  “sympathy for those affected” if approached by another third party and “let’s follow the protocol” in your first response. The choice of first phrase shows compassion while very specifically not choosing a side. The second phrase serves three important purposes:
  1. It reminds people aware of The Trust Protocol to check their own gut reactions against the guidelines, and reminds everyone to present a measured and united front.
  2. It puts everyone who sees the message on notice that derailment tactics, sock-puppeting, manipulation, and harassment probably won’t work. The community is not going to take the bait. A match will burn out for lack of oxygen.
  3. It prompts people who aren’t aware of The Trust Protocol to ask “what is that?”, which creates an avenue for The Trust Protocol to spread and gain wider adoption.

  1. If you want to show some support with #1 above, you are NOT required to go any further. A simple response with the two key phrases in #1 is a neutral show of support. No one has a right to ask you to go further and stick your own neck out. Everyone should understand that some leftist voices might want to help with a situation, but still might need to abstain from involvement for reasons of personal well-being. Therefore, we all should strive to resist the temptation to drag other people into a situation especially though a claim of guilt by inaction.

  1. You should understand the risks before engaging beyond #2 as a bystander.  Are you willing to hear or see things that might be unpleasant or triggering in some way? Are you willing to deal with an emotionally volatile situation- including some angry words or accusatory sentiments thrown in your direction too? Make sure you have the energy and fortitude to see it through. You should familiarize yourself with what resources are available as soon as you read this passage. If you find yourself in the position of wanting to support someone, Emergent crisis is not the first time you want to be trying to find resources. Know that publicly committing yourself (and in online spaces, the audience), may be putting more fuel on the fire, and that you can come under attack from it.
    Please note that the references to ‘emotionally volatile situations’ is not intended to excuse harassment or abusive behavior sent to bystanders- there is a distinct difference between an intemperate statement made in a heated moment and harassment.

  1. Ask them what they need. If someone has come to you in distress it may be difficult for you to work out what they want, or why they are telling you. Remember that distressed people do not always communicate in a generally expected or predictable way, so ask them what they need and be patient. You cannot be required to do anything, but you can’t make and informed decision if you don’t know what is being asked for. The person might not want you to do anything for them, and assuming they do may escalate the situation.

  1. Assess whether you’re willing and able to provide what you’ve been asked for. You might want to consider the following questions:
  1. Do you have the power to do what’s being asked of you?
  2. Do you have the emotional and mental capacity to do what’s being asked of you?
  3. Do you have the emotional and mental capacity to assess whether this is something you are willing or able to provide?
  4. Do you have enough information to feel comfortable doing what’s been asked of you?
  5. Are you being asked to take sides, especially without a clear understanding of the situation?

  1. If you find that you cannot provide what you have been asked for, ask if they would like you to direct them somewhere else they may be able to get the support or accommodation they need. Getting support can be a difficult process for people in distress, try to remain patient and compassionate even if they become frustrated that you are unable to help. Check if they have a support network or close friends they could confide in. Have a look at our Resources documen[ap]t and direct them to somewhere or someone else who might be able to help them more than you can.

  1. Resist the urge to go beyond expressing sympathy and a call to “follow the protocol” unless you are specifically invited to help mediate by the people directly involved in the claim. Many of us see a comrade in pain or under attack and feel a natural urge to jump in and help. Consider that  in many cases “jumping in to help” uninvited can make the situation worse, especially if your sudden presence makes one or both sides feel attacked.
  2. Prioritize genuine care for all victims first and foremost. A good-faith accuser is a victim of some type of harm, even if the person they’re accusing is ultimately not responsible for that harm. And of course the accused is subject to an entirely different harm of reputation or worse. If you don’t have enough information, strive to maintain neutral language and redirect conversation away from who’s right or wrong and instead to the subject of mitigating harm produced in the dispute, and supporting the healing process for those harmed. What exactly can be done right now to reduce any harm already done and prevent more harm from occurring? A common type of goalpost-moving that will happen even in a good-faith situation is one or both sides lashing out at the other with recriminations about who’s to blame. Again, you can use “let’s stay on protocol” and point out that prioritizing immediate care always comes first.
  3. Avoid any type of victim blaming. The person who comes forward should be treated with respect and care, their story listened to in a non-judgmental fashion, and excessive or inappropriate questioning (why did you wait a week before coming forward?[aq] Why did you go out with X person alone? etc) avoided. To this end, think about what kind of questions you can ask that can understand the victim’s safety and needs better, like “are you safe right now?”. A victim should never feel that they will be subjected to embarrassment or scrutiny on anything not directly related to their situation - such as their personal life and relationships, history of mental illness, disability (particularly cognitive or intellectual) etc. 
  4. Any situation with an imbalance of power should have that imbalance acknowledged and considered.  Knowing that power imbalances can cause coercive and abusive situations to arise that might not be visible to the abuser is and important thing to take note of when understanding the nature of the violation. The accountability requirements for an abuse of power will likely be different for a victim, and requirements should be placed as necessary to address this on top of the other components of accountability for the violation.
  5. At NO time should anyone involved be harassed, dogpiled, or threatened. This type of punitive behavior runs counter to the spirit of restorative justice and is harmful. It invites escalation and the piling on of “trolls” or bad faith actors and should be condemned.
  6. Canned non-involvement: A canned initial response and a canned reply response for the protocol for when you’re being coerced or harassed. The initial response might not be enough for either emotionally engaged people or bad actors. Another set of canned phrases will allow anyone familiar with the protocol to know that you are being coerced or harassed. Neutral, Standardized language will defuse good faith attention and disempower bad faith attacks. The assumption in these phrases is that you are not being contacted by the claimant themself.
  1. For a standard post after coercion has commenced:
    “I have been asked to (do something) and i do not have the expertise to understand the situation. Though I have sympathy for those affected i have to sit out, please abide the trust principle.”
  2. And for a reply:
    “I’ve chosen to sit out, please respect the trust principle and my decision.

  1. If the claimant is contacting you while you are being coerced or harassed about your decision ask them to intercede on your behalf while you find the time to make an informed decision.
  2. To whatever extent is possible, focus on the present and the future. You may never truly know what happened between two people but you can know that harm has been experienced by one or more parties. Focus on ways to resolve continuing harm rather than trying to determine the ‘facts’ of the situation. What can be known is what people need now and going forward to be safe and supported enough to heal and/or be accountable for their actions and prevent them from occurring again. 
  3. While conflict or harassment is ongoing, try to keep people experiencing harm or triggers from coming into conflict with one another. The reason that one person might be willing to coerce or harass another is because of their past trauma, this might also be the reason why someone is refraining from being involved. When people are emotionally vulnerable, misunderstandings, which are likely to occur anyway, often keep the conflict going and grow it and extend its harm to more people.
  4. Punishment is not the goal, do not approach the issue with punishment in mind. The safety of the victim and the relevant communities come first. Accountability, Reconciliation and rehabilitation come second. Punishment does not support these objectives.

Protocol C - If YOU feel tangibly harmed by the situation resulting from a damaging claim (e.g. targeted harassment)

The guidelines in Protocol G, Protocol H and Protocol X are generally applicable to this situation too.

Being targeted by harassment is never the fault of the person being harassed. While there is no objectively “right” way to handle harassment, here are some things that might be helpful to you. Take care of your own safety and well-being first.

  1. Consider reaching out to someone (a trusted friend, family member, counselor) who can offer emotional support and constructive advice. These confidantes can provide you with help while you are being subjected to a harassment campaign.
  2. Consider taking steps to limit people’s effective engagement with you such as turning off open DMs, turning off anonymous questions on curious cat, opt out of notifications on all your affected social media, and turning off replies from people who don’t follow you on Twitter, as well as other precautions in the same vein.
  3. Mute and block buttons are your friend. Harassers will use a block a point of pride often by posting a picture of the block, but they have no ground to stand on, having harassed you.

  1. Consider refraining from responding to harassment. While it’s entirely understandable you would    seek to defend yourself, should you notice harassment merely persists and increases when you do so, it may be beneficial to disengage. Often when you have come under harassment the stress can cause you to say things you do not believe or are harmful but that you can’t perceive as wrong for whatever reason. You may be may be attacked for poorly worded, or emotionally charged  statements. On top of this you’ll have to be accountable for the things you say during this period, which is an additional stressor that you don’t need while you are recovering from a harassment campaign.

  1. Consider disengaging from social media for a while if your mental health is significantly affected. It’s okay to take breaks when you need it, especially if you find yourself in a harmful situation, it doesn’t make you weak. On the contrary abstaining from social media is a difficult task at the best of times, and doing so while being harassed is significantly moreso.


Protocol D - If someone has made a damaging claim about the accuracy/fairness of your published content

The guidelines in Protocol B, Protocol E, Protocol F ,and Protocol X are generally applicable to this situation too.

  1. Assume people are criticising your content in good faith and refrain from knee-jerk reactions. Take the time to reflect and see if you can understand where people are coming from. If you feel like there are points that are fair and which you can concede to, acknowledge them and do so immediately through the medium you were contacted on, and consider adding an addendum to either another video or the description of your video.
  2. If you feel criticism is not justified or stems from a misunderstanding of your position, you can politely provide clarifications or elaboration. Refrain from overly defensive or aggressive language, maintaining a neutral posture especially by asking for clarification or sources as necessary may help diffuse misunderstandings, and inform you better on the circumstances themselves.
  3. When you respond, acknowledge the effort people have made to engage with and reflect on your content. [ar]It is important to note that many claims, especially claims in response to long form content, are going to be made with the best of intentions, whether or not they are valid, it is important to try to understand why the person thought they were valid, and to make sure the person understands that you are not dismissing their concerns.
  4. Chances are you will not see eye to eye with the people criticising your content. There’s little point in an endless cycle of expressing the same disagreement. After clarifications, sources, and other rationale are produced, if you find that you don’t accept their criticism, Drop the issue, because it is no longer constructive. Consider muting the thread.

Protocol E - If you are a content creator or social commentator being asked by others to weigh in on a damaging claim

All the guidelines in Protocol B are generally applicable to this situation too.

  1. De-escalation of harassment and brigading: A person with a large follower base has to consider potential benefits and consequences of weighing in responsibly and their choice needs to be respected. Resolution and accountability processes rarely benefit from large numbers of uninvolved people jumping in. To this end if a person has come to be harassed, critique may have to wait until after the situation has been resolved in order to not provoke further harassment. To this end, as a person with a large audience don’t ‘punch down’ with your content unless you’re willing to have the results on your conscience, and face accountability for harm it may cause.

  1. A response to a given conflict should not be demanded from anyone. If you think that a third party should weigh in that is your right, but they may feel too unqualified to speak on such a conflict, have unknown triggers, or feel unsafe to talk about it in a given setting for whatever reason. Don’t make content attempting to coerce that person into involving themself.
  2. If someone is caught up in the conflict and then chooses to stop, it does not then permit you to harass them in private conversations. One may choose to stop participating because it is negatively affecting their health or the events being discussed may be too triggering for them to continue. Don’t take a change in involvement as a reason to guilt them in the content you make.
  3. Even if you’re not directly involved in the matter, don’t mention it on social media, videos, or livestreams. Avoid mentioning there is a concerning situation if at all possible, but if you consent after being pressured, or to prevent hearsay or victim blaming, only mention that an internal discussion with several parties is ongoing. Do not engage further, and do not use stress as a reason to produce content that may escalate a situation.
  4. People reaching out for your opinion are doing so in good faith. Don’t lash out, please realize that these most of these people aren’t attacking you, and aren’t reaching out to you just for attention. If people begin to harass you, don’t directly engage them because you may inadvertently send some of the people who see your content to harass them. It may feel like you are letting yourself get walked all over, but you are responsible for your platform and the harm it may cause.

Protocol F - If you are a content creator or social commentator voluntarily deciding to publish commentary about a claim for which you are a third party

All the guidelines in Protocol B, Protocol E ,and Protocol Z are generally applicable to this situation too.

  1. Don’t attempt to generate social capital for yourself. The spectacle of the a resolution process which has turned into a discourse alienates and harms those who are actually involved in the resolution process. Strive to wait until after the dust has settled on the issue. The most insightful commentary will be a retrospective, and allowing more time to take notes and revise your assessments will only help your insights. Trying to do even a neutral commentary while the situation is hot creates too many temptations for other third parties to act in a way that will add fuel to the fire.

    If you do have an insight that makes it difficult to resist the temptation to produce content while the situation is still ongoing, you should strive to keep such commentary as abstract as possible and avoid naming specific actors. The insights you might have about these dynamics are just as valid without naming names. Remember that every commentary that is levied at a specific person that is being dogpiled can add to the pressure and harm they might already be experiencing.  The tendency to prolong these conflicts (especially between third parties) tends to only exacerbate collateral damage.

    As for determining when, exactly, a situation has been resolved, potential indicators are typically when the claimant and accused have publicly made some definitive statements or the discourse has stopped talking about the issue for a good number of days. If in doubt, simply wait a bit longer!
  2. Do not claim that 3rd party bystanders are guilty by association or guilty by inaction for things done prior to knowledge of any claims. Most leftist voices know and interact with each other peripherally at best, in parasocial relationships. Remember that some people have their own history and triggers, so it might simply be painful—or harmful—to insert themselves into the situation, particularly when an accountability process is ongoing.
  3. Avoid using a harsh or critical attitude towards any person directly involved in the damaging claim. Saying harsh things to or about the victim may cause a secondary trauma and they may be less likely than ever to continue to speak up. This chilling effect leads other people who have experienced similar harm to decide not to come forward if they feel the environment is hostile. This supports rape culture and allows abusers to harm more people. All care should be taken to avoid such a toxic environment. Serious issues like rape and abuse should never be dismissed as “drama”, “in-fighting” or terms of that nature, harassment shouldn’t be dismissed as “discourse”. Additionally after witnessing an uncharitable version of their position from an uninvolved commentator, a compliant accused person complying with what’s required to reach accountability for their actions might become indignant and unwilling to continue, and the denial of accountability will harm the victim who needs it.
  4. It is good praxis to evaluate serious events in hindsight with the cooperation of the people directly involved in the claim. After a reasonable period of time has taken place, there is a definite benefit to performing a retrospective with all members of a group empowered to comment on their experiences—especially those who were harmed. A frank and productive postmortem comprising  mistakes made, improvements needed, as well how the agreed upon path was implemented and what was successful.


Protocol G - If you are experiencing direct threats about your real-world environment

The guidelines in Protocol C and Protocol H are generally applicable to this situation too.

  1. Disengage. If someone is threatening your safety in the real world, report them on the social media platform and cease interaction to include blocking them.

Protocol H - If you are experiencing physical or emotional harm in your real-world environment

The guidelines in Protocol C and Protocol G are generally applicable to this situation too.

  1. If one or more persons involved in a conflict are unable to access care for their basic needs (defined broadly as food, shelter, medication, and immediate safety for themselves and/or their family) it is within this person’s best interest to identify and address these needs before attempting to resolve the conflict. If the person or persons are unable to access this care directly related to the current conflict, community support systems or other resources must be identified before attempting to address or resolve the conflict. This is only applicable under temporary conditions or conditions related directly to an event or conflict, they are not intended to solve long-term or past access needs. Please refer to the resources (link here)

  1. The safety of people comes first. No one should be judged for seeking the help of law enforcement if this is what they need to do to protect themselves.
  2. Have a way out. For these situations, you should prepare or look for what you need to remove yourself if things escalate to a dangerous degree. Try to find trusted friends or potential shelters to stay at.

Protocol X - If you have been harmed and need to hold someone accountable for that harm

  1. Ask for help. You are entitled to decide the manner by which this is brought to attention. 
  2. Decide whether you think the harm will need a formal process of accountability. In many cases, the person who caused harm is willing to be responsible for their actions and hold themselves accountable to you directly. If this is not possible due to the severity of the harm or other complications, a formal process is more appropriate.
  3. Form a pod and request that the person who did harm do the same. For more information on how to form a pod, see The Trust Protocol Resource Page (link here).
  4. Media Silence. Once pods have formed, refrain from publicly speaking out against the person who has done harm before they have had an opportunity to hold themselves accountable. Ask the people who have reached out to support to do the same.
  5. Make specific demands for accountability. Work with your pod to determine how to best approach this. See The Trust Protocol Resource Page for support in developing ways to communicate your needs and examples of what that could look like.
  6. Escalation. If escalation and/or publicity within your accountability process is agreed to be necessary, it should always keep harm reduction in focus and should never be designed to inflict harm. Ask yourself if taking steps to escalate will result in a different outcome or if the motivation is simple vengeance.
  7. Reconciliation. If the person who caused harm is able to grow and learn from the harm they have caused, you or your pod should consider acknowledging that growth. If the harm was made public or done publicly, you or your pod might consider making accountability results or efforts equally public.
  8. Aftermath. Once you’ve reached a reconciliation or it is clear that no amount of further escalation will work to hold the person who caused harm more accountable, consider focusing on more personal self care measures. Continue to seek support from close relationships.

Protocol Y - If you are an appointed moderator in a moderated space or are a self-organizing member of a leftist space

The guidelines in Protocol B are generally applicable to this situation too.

  1. Depending on the nature of the accusation the group may collectively decide that they believe reconciliation is possible. First and foremost, the needs and feelings of the victim should be centered; if the victim expresses that they are unable to feel safe in the same group, are too traumatized to handle the situation, etc, then this should be respected without judgment. However if the victim is willing, and there are volunteers available to facilitate, then the group may seek a reconciliation.

  1. Strive to have a set of prepared links or materials about support groups that you can give to the people directly involved in the incident . Not only is good material on how to grow and and heal difficult to come by, but when it damages your private spaces it feels terrible. If a member of your community is leaving regardless of whether it is a claimant or an accused person, or why they are leaving, as a moderator you should give them some information that helps them, since good material is so hard to find. If there have been public mentions of some health concern by them remind them that if they are able they should visit their healthcare provider or to seek other trained care to help overcome contributing factors which might have a physiological component.
  2. It should be understood that all members of a group will be affected emotionally by the situation, as they may feel fear, disappointment, betrayal, etc. The emotional impact is valid, and they should feel free to discuss their experiences within the group so long as this does not in any way expose the victim to more harm or makes them unsafe. In terms of public statements they need to consider statements carefully to diminish the risk of bringing bad faith actors into the situation, and if the group collectively chooses to avoid public statements during sensitive periods of the accountability process this should be respected.
  3. Server managers (discord, slack, etc) who adopt the protocol will designate spaces where venting can be done to include uncharitable or invective things and those spaces will be clearly marked to avoid confusion and unintentional slander. Moderators of those servers should work to keep venting entirely restricted to these spaces and to keep the tone of the conversation from reaching libellous or threatening levels. In those conversations and spaces moderators should work to keep sarcasm and glibness to a minimum, humor can cut the tension but facetiousness won’t. Additionally, things said in the implied confidence of a venting space should not be taken out of the context of that space.
  4. Building a support network: For public spaces a role or shorthand indicator should be set up to indicate a person who has experience and training handling crisis, and who is safe to talk to, and willing to be that person. Because of the nature of social media and of crisis these people should be willing to be available at any time, or to list the hours they’re available. These people should be versed in this protocol as well as whatever other forms of investigation and resolution processes they feel comfortable with being responsible for. People taking on this role must understand that they are going to likely be exposed to graphic depictions of abuse and will need to be both willing to be exposed, and prepared for that. These people will afford confidentiality to the victim until the victim wants to take another path, and will be willing to research and help the victim through official accountability processes appropriate for their region.

 Protocol Z - If you are an active member in a leftist space who witnessed the situation resulting from a damaging claim

The guidelines in Protocol B are generally applicable to this situation too.

  1. A community that has gone through an internal ordeal, has done all that they can to resolve situations: reviewed practices, admitted and apologized for errors and engaged in healing, engaged in reconciliation and restoration for the victim should then consider the situation resolved. It should not be something that individual members should drag out again in future situations “Oh X is such garbage, you remember what they did that time…” This is an unacceptable bad faith tactic.
  2. Often, victims will want to maintain as much of their privacy as they can, so victims of abuse should expect confidence and privacy from the people they reach out to, until they are ready to come forward with their claims. This kind of private conversation to aid the healing of the victim should not be used to construe that the confidante was hiding evidence in order to support an abuser. In such a situation if the confidante has been a confidante of other people abused by the same person, they can say that without naming the victims unless they have been cleared by those victims. The support and information that a victim receives from their confidantes shouldn’t be used publicly without the consent of the those confidantes.
  3. We should not rely on whisper campaigns, A whisper campaign is where individuals use direct messaging, private conversations, and other means of direct communication between small privileged groups to disseminate sensitive, often damaging information. As often as not they are used to harm victims instead of to prevent victimization, and because of their clandestine nature it is impossible to know who has been informed. This leads to a no-responsibility situation where a poor uniformity of information makes some people get bombarded by the campaign and others left uninformed and vulnerable while completely avoiding accountability processes for the accused.
  4. Comfort in, dump out. If you are experiencing emotional upset, triggers, or secondary trauma but are not at the centre of the conflict, process your negative feelings with people who are further removed from the heart of the conflict than you are. Your feelings are important and real, but they may contribute to overwhelm and/or the emotional burden of people who are being more directly harmed. Remember to protect the confidence and privacy of victims by avoiding names and details and focusing instead on your own feelings.
  5. Do not use accusations as a jumping off point for any bones you might have to pick with ANYONE involved. During this process is not the time to come at people for their political tendency, or other non-harmful online behaviour you might dislike. This only opens the door for personal attacks and harassment. Additionally, the claims and accountability you may want to make for other harmful behaviors should be done in the same way as other claims.
  6. Do not speculate and discourage speculation in others. It may be tempting to use prior experience with abuse cases to make assumptions about what people involved have said and done. Please stick to what you know has been said/done so as to not muddy the discussion or slander involved parties.

  7. To generally avoid all “character building” tactics: When an accusation comes to light and there is no conclusive evidence, the adherents of this protocol should generally avoid strategies that involve “building a character profile”. This includes behaviours that aren’t related to the accusation but associate negative characteristics to any person involved. This doesn’t help with the case in particular and is often abused. It’s also commonly used to silence victims or pressure victims into not speaking up in the first place. It’s important to remember that a person you personally think is “bad” may still be victims of abuse.

  1. If the victim agrees to a resolution, accept it. Do not drag up their trauma at a later date to attack the accused for unrelated reasons if the victim has agreed that the matter is resolved.
  2. All public statements should be made with accommodations to disabled people. This includes no minimization of terms commonly mocked like “triggers” or “spoons” and including trigger warnings in public announcements regarding to the affair.
  3. Don’t pester creators about their opinion on the matter. The need for care and privacy of those involved should be respected and content creators should be free to decide whether or not they should weigh in, especially when the situation is not yet resolved or understood.
  4. Don’t feel the need to rush to the defense of a person or platform you like. The nature of parasocial relationships, especially when you’ve financially invested in the other party via Patreon, buying merch, or books, can be intense. It is important to note that criticism of a platformed person is not a criticism of you.
  5. These times can be sensitive. Please be civil in all engagements. There is no excuse for using opposing views as a jumping off point for victim blaming, racism, xenophobia, homophobia, ableism, anti-semitism, Islamophobia, transphobia, or serophobia.
  6. Wanting a different method of resolution doesn’t make someone an authoritarian. Don’t call people “cops”, “KGB”, “SS”, “FBI”, “CIA”, or any other term with similar connotations.
  7. Resist the temptation dismiss any criticism out of hand, or to paint anyone involved in the situation as a bad actor. It’s best to assume that people on both sides are operating in good faith unless there are clear signs otherwise. Don’t call them “social capitalists” or say they’re disingenuous by calling them a psyop from outside communities such as KiwiFarms or 4Chan
  8. Throughout any incident of confrontation, accusation, mediation, and resolution all involved should be treated as human beings. Human beings are imperfect, unique, and not disposable. Language that paints a dichotomy of othering and lessening the value of people involved should be avoided to help prevent the mindset of “us and them”/”with or against”

Part 2 - Ethical Principles

As per our general recommendations to all communities, the procedures and guidelines outlined in the protocol, shall be considered as governed by the following principles:

  1. Respect to human dignity: we believe all communities should recognize self-determination, association, personal privacy, inviolability of correspondence and free development of personality. Online and offline, we must recognize one another as individuals and Ends-in-Themselves.
     
  2. Due diligence: procedures and guidelines should be carried out according to the terms outlined in the present Protocol, or any that a community might develop by themselves. Acting shall be in due diligence as an obligation of the corresponding ‘authorities’[at][au][av], whichever might constitute the community over which they hold responsibility. The procedure as such should be characterized by reasonable prevention, exhaustive investigation, proportional sanctions and due reparations.
  3. Confidentiality:[aw] any and all revelations of personal information are to be limited to the people involved in the procedure, and only in respect of what they might need to know. Therefore, ‘authorities’ and involved parties should preserve the privacy of personal information along all steps of procedure with the intention of protecting a person’s privacy. The complexity and inherent dubiousness of an online society may always run the risk of misinformation being spread, and we must try to deter bad-faith actors and opportunists from acting thoughtlessly seeing these processes as nothing but resources.
  4. Impartiality: independently of any qualifications upon a particular denouncement, any and all people are to be treated in the same manner of respect and non-discrimination. Participants should not be considered biased after the fact, and ‘guilt by association’ should always be deterred from taking root Just as well, communities and individuals can exercise their rights to the aforementioned free association and simply decide to not associate with a particular individual.[ax][ay][az]
  5. Accessibility:[ba] procedures should be materially available for any and all people and these shall participate in equal conditions. Just as well, all participation should be considered voluntary from the beginning. An unwillingness to participate in the resolution of a conflict or the discussion of a tragic event should not be seen as being complicit with any act of abuse. Additionally, an open working group might consider producing reports on the situation that others may refer to for recommendations, instead of expecting any and all members of a particular community to look into the entirety of the case themselves.
  6. No Revictimization: to avoid giving third-parties the opportunity to benefit themselves, the victim shall be treated with respect to their way of being, feeling, thinking and acting, without any mistreatment or differential treatment. More so, having them narrate their story to different people and instances should be avoided, unless strictly necessary or done voluntarily. There is no need to further deliberate over specifics that might cause psychological harm to the particular individuals. Furthermore, continued conversation on such topics can border on harassment for the victim, for which they may naturally lash out.
  7. Transparency: all procedure and their results should be clearly explained to the parties involved, and all actions should be clearly perceived by involved parties as for their benefit.
  8. Reconciliation: procedures that a particular community might exercise should be carried out with the ultimate goal of reconciliation, although not between victim and perpetrator, necessarily. A particular victim's input as to the goal of the Protocol implementation should always be taken into account.  The effort of limiting and preventing harm is paramount, and revenge goes against this particular principle. As such it is recognized that victims come first, accountability and growth of the abuser comes second. Actions are to be taken toward the goal of safety, not retribution. Accountability should be incentivized through collective action with the goal of producing empathetic community members in mind.
  9. Honesty: [bb][bc][bd][be]Open and honest communications are important. People should feel that they are able to express what they need and be heard.
  10. Accountability: none of the above principles should be construed as shielding abusers and perpetrators from responsibility, nor that cases of abuse should be kept secret as an appropriate measure. Instead, we present these suggestions merely as guidelines that communities might adopt to help them benefit survivors and give them the appropriate space and time to heal.
  11. The Protocol should radically promote the concept of reconciliation as a goal where safe and appropriate. Change your mindset away from punishment. The purpose of this protocol is to limit and prevent harm. Keep in mind that the instinct for revenge will not accomplish less harm and is in fact a method of control. It is normal to want to regain control in a situation where someone has been harmed, but exerting violent control over the accused (or anyone willing to listen to them) will only alienate them from the process of accountability (exceptions being if the abuse is ongoing or likely to imminently reoccur; The safety of the victims must be established before the accountability process can begin). Therefore, any actions taken by the community should be done in the spirit of safety and preventing harm. This means that the victims come first,  then accountability and growth of the abuser. This does not mean that we do not take action against someone who is causing harm. Simply that our actions must be toward the goal of safety and not retribution. This allows consequences of abusive actions to occur naturally, ie: an abuser may no longer be allowed access to spaces where they have perpetrated harm until a time if and when the members of that space (most importantly, their victim(s) within that space) have fully reconciled with the abuser and are fully satisfied that they have changed*. This mindset will also serve to discourage harassment campaigns against people who are not directly involved with the conflict since policing opinions on a matter will not increase the safety of anyone involved.

    Also, a reconciliation mindset will create space for abusers to take accountability for their actions and incentivise them to do the work required to become better people and more empathetic community members. We need to be open to the fact that people can and do change, and that perpetual condemnation will galvanize abusive behaviour where reconciliation can lead to stronger community and a wealth of learning and growth. So if the accused is willing to take accountability for the harm they have caused, we must be willing to facilitate reconciliation where possible(if the victim is unwilling to participate, they must not be pressured into it!). This process must be done voluntarily and in good faith by the accused, if it has been determined that harm has been done, and those who have perpetrated harm are unwilling to accept accountability and progress in good faith, then they remain a danger to the community until they do so. *See list of red flags to help weed out bad faith actors attempting to manipulate the process of reconciliation in order to regain access to their victims.[bf][bg]
  12. We shouldn’t consider people to be cancelled or exiled from the community[bh][bi][bj][bk] simply because they don’t want to enact recommendations from working group. In other words, if person X is problematic and the working group recommends excluding them from our spaces, but person A wants to keep person X in their community, we may need to then have further discussions as to whether we can agree to disagree or if there’s a safety concern that impacts the whole community needing everyone to act (and what the consequences of not acting on those recommendations should be - eg. if someone is an abuser and needs to be removed from everywhere, what do we do about creators / community activists who refuse to remove them?).

    Trust that principle Adopters of the protocol will respect the choice that other adopters have made, while affording them the space to operate or privacy from those proceedings. This will ensure that all effort is made toward helping the victim heal, and prevent the wasted emotional labor of trying to compel assistance from those wishing to be uninvolved. Adopters of the protocol will also respect the choices of those who choose not to adopt the protocol, though harassment is not acceptable in any case.
  13. Believe survivors. A person’s account of trauma is their personal truth. Trauma cannot be verified or disproven within a trial based framework, it simply is.
  14. Everyone has a right to heal. Healing from trauma is an inalienable and fundamental human right.
  15. Everyone has the capacity to grow. We must not mistake reluctance or unwillingness to accept change at any particular time as a lack of ability to do so.
  16. Hurt people hurt people[bl][bm][bn]. Harmful behavior is not a measure of the quality of a person. It is the product of trauma and the toxic systems we are forced to navigate. While this does not excuse abuse, it is vital to recognize that abuse is a symptom of something greater than any one individual.
  17. Remember that abuse may not be clear cut. Sometimes two people have harmed one another. Both parties deserve love and support from those who care about them.
  18. Harm reduction, not elimination. We can reduce the harm caused by others by removing people who are not ready or able to hold themselves accountable from positions of influence whenever possible. We can refuse to promote harmful methods of deplatforming such as threats of violence and doxxing. We cannot, however, eliminate a person from the internet or completely control any particular narrative. Nor can we force a person who is unwilling or unable to hold themselves accountable to any standard of accountability. Harm is, unfortunately, an inevitability that we can simply do our best to cope and support each other with.[bo][bp][bq][br]
  19. There should be a designated way for any member(s) of leftist spaces to bring forward concerns, esp concerns of feeling unsafe or a toxic environment.. Collective and democratic problem solving should be the norm. There should never be any reprisals for anyone communicating these concerns, and they should be able to do so confidentially (the only reason for breaking confidentiality would be if there were an imminent threat of harm, such as an actionable threat of one member against another).
  20. Any position in a space which give people authority over others (such as mod) is a privilege. There needs to be a designated way to bring forward requests to remove authority from such a person if it is necessary (such as holding a vote of no-confidence, etc).
  21. The rights of all people need to be upheld, and any actions which are discriminatory or violate the dignity of another group (use of slurs, etc) should not be tolerated. Should members of a minority or marginalized group bring forward concerns particular to their identity this should be seen as an opportunity to create a better space for all, not a grievance or bother. Any decision that involves a certain group should have members of the group given a voice: “nothing about us without us”.[bs][bt]
  22. Ableism, sexism, etc has no place in these conversations. A person with a history of mental illness should not be treated as less reliable as to their testimonials, a male victim should not be disregarded as a victim because of the myth that men cannot be raped, etc. (It is certainly acceptable for people to bring up such things in terms of assisting the person to give the best opportunity to bring forward issues- such as asking if they need assistance with an interpreter if english is not their first language or if they require breaks and rest if they have an anxiety disorder, however this must be done in an attitude of helpfulness and with care and sensitivity).
  23. An ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure. For instance, it is strongly recommended that trainings or information on consent be made available to people. People should also educate themselves on rape myths and facts (please see Protocol Zero).
    This general principle of making information available to people in order to prevent issues can be broadly applied to multiple situations.

There is likely to be doubt unless there is a confession or some extraordinary piece of evidence is found. In cases like this, it is the safety of people that is the utmost concern, and a leftist group must move to protect vulnerable individuals and exercise reasonable judgment. Multiple accusations of harassment or stalking, for instance, may well be considered a reason for dismissal from a group even if absolute proof is not something it would be reasonable to expect a mod of an online group to be able to procure. Wherever possible, democratic principles should apply to decisions of this magnitude.

  • Admitting to and accepting full responsibility for what they have done

(This section is not meant to be directly part of The Trust Protocol)

=================

Workgroup Note: The following list was originally proposed by @Allie as candidates for the “actionable guidelines” in the various protocol sections. But some discussion in the #review-comments channel of the Discord made clear that the following steps were really more the start of a full “Accountability Process” that The Protocol might recommend in various actionable guidelines.  This is a great idea, but trying to shoehorn a complete methodology into a categorized set of actionable guidelines for different situations is not the best way to present this type of information/instruction. (In part because this type of “accountability process” could be equally useful for a variety of different actionable guidelines.

Therefore, we have two choices: We either A) simply find some existing sites/orgs that teach some type of “accountability process” and simply reference them in the supplemental “Resources” page that accompanies The Trust Protocol, or we B) author our own unique version of an accountability process (suitable for online spaces and individual volunteers) and put it in another supplemental page named something like “How to conduct an accountability process in online spaces”. Of course, the problem is that somebody would need to step up and lead a sub-team to actually write a page like this.

=================

 

  1. Harm is brought to attention. This could be a new incident or a disclosure of past harm. The survivor is entitled to decide the manner by which this is brought to attention.
  2. Pods are formed. The accuser and accused both need teams of people in their “camp”.[cz]
  • Pods should be comprised of people who are already familiar with the person in question and people they can trust. Failing that, they should form around a small team of people who can listen to the person in question without judgement.
  • Pod activity should be confidential and invite-only. [This is the type of venting space Tyr proposed] The person for whom the pod is formed should have the final say in who is invited.
  • A pod’s only objective is to support the person in question. For the survivor, this might look like affirming the survivor’s narrative, supporting them to seek professional supports such as counseling, acting as a public mouthpiece, etc. For the accused, this might look like helping them unpack why their behavior was harmful, supporting them to seek professional supports, being available for accountability check ins, etc.
  1. Media Silence. Once pods have formed, both parties should refrain from speaking publicly on the matter. While it may be tempting for the accused to defend themself, that hasn’t been asked of them or consented to at this time. Nor is it acceptable for the accuser to continue to publicly speak out against the accused before they have had an opportunity to hold themself accountable.
  2. The Accountability Ask. The survivor’s pod asks the survivor, “What does accountability look like in this situation?” and “What would you need to feel as safe as possible?”. The pod then works with the survivor to formulate a concrete set of requests from the other pod. Examples of asks:
  • Admit that harm was done.
  • Agree to never communicate with me again.
  • Remove yourself from [online group].
  • Agree to attend 12 Step Meetings/ a Health Realization program/ stay sober
  • Have regular meetings with your support system
  1. The Accountability Response. The accused person’s pod asks the person, “Which of these terms are you ready or able to accept?” and “What is your timeline for doing so?”. The pod then works with the accused person to formulate a response. It is important to note that not all of the accountability asks may necessarily be realistic or feasible. For example, if the ask is that the accused person sees a professional counselor once a week, the person might not have access to affordable health insurance. If the ask would result in the loss of the accused’s income, they might have a moral objection to that if other people rely on that income. The accused is entitled to decline any of the terms for any reason, understanding that they may face consequences for doing so.
  2. Escalation. As a matter of good faith, and keeping with the principle of harms reduction, the accuser’s pod should initially assume that the accused has every intention to comply with the accountability asks. However, as the accountability process progresses and the willingness of the other person becomes more apparent, escalated measure may be deemed necessary on the part of the pod. For example, the accuser initially wishes to keep the accountability process confidential, but when the accused refuses to admit harm the pod agrees to make the process public. Escalation should always keep harms reduction in the forefront, and should never be designed to inflict harm. The pod must continue to ask itself if taking steps to escalate will result in a different outcome or if the motivation is simply vengeance. When it becomes clear that the accused is not going to move any further toward accountability, escalation is no longer useful. Survivors will continue to need support, and it is important for them to find a different means to heal, and/or reconcile with the accused person on the points of accountability on which they agree to cooperate.  

  1. Reconciliation. If the accused is willing and able to follow through with the accountability asks, the two people in question are able to move toward a process of reconciliation. This will look different for every case. As an accused person is able to grow and learn from the harm they have caused, that growth should be recognized by the survivor and/or their pod. If the accused person was removed from certain spaces because they posed a threat, it might be deemed that they are no longer the threat they once were and they can be welcomed back in. If the harm was made public, the efforts that an accused person made should be made equally public.

  1. Aftermath. Conflicts and harmful behavior between two people will never reach a complete conclusion. From the moment trauma is experienced, it lives on in the bodies and minds of those who experience it. And while accountability is sometimes a simple and straightforward process, sometimes it spans over the course of a person’s lifetime. With that in mind, it conflicts do tend to reach an equilibrium after a formal accountability process has been attempted. This will happen once there is a reconciliation, or when it is clear that no amount of further escalation will work to hold the accused person more accountable. Pods may wish to stop convening formally, but it is nonetheless important for both people involved to continue to receive love and support from those who care about them, no matter the outcome. At this point in time, when the conflict is mentioned publicly, it is important to refer back to the outcome.

Emerican’s terminology of social spaces[da]

A "community" is a place where all members are at least personally aware of each other and there is at least some degree of intimacy. Real communities are difficult to build online due to some inherent problems related to anonymity, etc., though they do exist.

A "space[db][dc][dd]" is an online platform where people are concentrated based on a certain affinity/interest but there is no real intimacy and nobody can truly know who they are talking to and who is a good/bad actor. This includes the vast majority of Discord servers, Facebook pages, subreddits, etc. Real life examples would include a hangout that is associated with a certain identity (i.e., bars, skate parks, cafes, and other hangout areas that are publicly accessible)

A "platform demographic[de][df]" is the very broad assemblage of all people of a certain identity/affinity on a particular platform, i.e. "Leftist Twitter" or "Leftbook" or "Breadtube"

A "movement" is the extremely broad assembly of all people around the world identifying with a particular social or political cause, i.e. "the LGBTQ+ movement" or "the leftist movement" or "the anarchist movement."
